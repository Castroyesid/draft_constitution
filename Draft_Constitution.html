A DRAFT FOR AN OPEN SOURCE CONSTITUTION

A 21st Century Social Contract

WE, THE PEOPLE, exercising our inherent sovereignty, do hereby establish this Constitution, with the aim of creating a more perfect Union, and invite all people to peacefully join our Union.

With this Constitution, we affirm our common wish to live together in peace and harmony, to preserve the heritage of the past, and to protect the promise of the future.

As people of many Nations, we cherish the diversity of our cultures; our differences enrich us.

We descend from the Indigenous Nations that sprang from these lands, and from the settlers who uprooted lives abroad to put down roots here.

We, who remain, wish no other home than this.

Having known war, we hope for peace; having been divided, we wish for unity. Having been ruled, we seek freedom.

With the goal of creating a living document that will grow and adapt to the world's ever-changing challenges, we offer this as an open source starting point.

ARTICLE I - Mission

Section 1.

The mission of this Constitution is to establish a government of, by, and for the People, founded on principles of direct democracy, shared sovereignty, ecological stewardship, and restorative justice.

Section 2.

This Constitution recognizes that:

  • (a) Sovereignty resides inherently in the People, who exercise it directly through participatory governance;
  • (b) Indigenous Nations possess rights arising from their ancestral connection to these lands, including co-governance and shared stewardship of territories;
  • (c) All people have equal dignity and rights under law;
  • (d) The land, waters, and air are held in common trust for present and future generations;
  • (e) Justice requires not only equality before the law, but active efforts to ensure equitable access to resources and opportunity.

Section 3.

This Constitution seeks to create a society where:

  • (a) Every person participates meaningfully in decisions that affect their lives;
  • (b) Power is distributed rather than concentrated;
  • (c) Economic security is guaranteed while extreme wealth inequality is addressed;
  • (d) Cultural diversity is celebrated and protected;
  • (e) The natural environment is preserved for those who will inherit it.

Section 4.

This Constitution is offered as an open source framework and vision of what is possible, inviting adaptation, improvement, and evolution in response to changing needs and growing wisdom. It is intended as a foundation for discussion and collaborative development, recognizing that transition from existing systems will require sustained organizing, movement building, and collective action.

ARTICLE II - Definitions

Section 1.

For purposes of this Constitution, the following terms shall have the meanings specified:

  • (a) Person means any human being.
  • (b) Protected Natural Entity means a river, waterway, lake, aquifer, forest, wetland, coral reef, mountain ecosystem, or other natural feature of ecological significance that has been designated for protection under Article VI. Protected Natural Entities possess legal rights as prescribed in Article IV, Section 13 and Article VI, and such rights shall be enforced through appointed guardians.
  • (c) Individual means a single person as distinguished from a group or organization.
  • (d) Registered Citizen means a person who has been entered into the Citizen Registry as prescribed in Article VIII.
  • (e) Citizen means a registered individual who has paid or had deducted the Annual Citizenship Tax for the current fiscal year, thereby confirming citizenship status.
  • (f) Neighborhood means a local governmental jurisdiction meeting the population and dwelling requirements prescribed in Article IX, Section 2.
  • (g) Municipality means a governmental jurisdiction composed of two or more neighborhoods as prescribed in Article IX, Section 3.
  • (h) Region means a governmental jurisdiction composed of two or more municipalities as prescribed in Article IX, Section 4.
  • (i) First Nations means those peoples with documented pre-colonial presence in the territories encompassed by this constitution.
  • (j) Latter Nations means cultural and political communities that emerged following colonial contact.
  • (k) Indigenous Nations means, collectively, both First Nations and Latter Nations when referenced in the context of political representation and voting.
  • (l) Appropriate Indigenous Nations means those Indigenous Nations with documented historical or ancestral connection to a specific territory, determined through self-assertion, historical treaties, archaeological evidence, oral tradition, and recognition by other Indigenous Nations, as prescribed in Article VII, Section 7(a).
  • (m) Traditional Leader means a person recognized by custom and tradition as holding a leadership role within their community, as prescribed in Article V.
  • (n) Federal Vote means a vote conducted among all citizens of the legal jurisdiction of this constitution through the secure digital voting platform or through offline voting facilities as prescribed in Article XI, Section 13, with results determined by the thresholds specified for each type of decision.
  • (o) Council of Representatives means a temporary body that may convene during extreme emergencies as prescribed in Article XII, Section 6.
  • (p) Legal Jurisdiction means the territorial extent and population over which this Constitution holds authority, comprising all Regions, Indigenous Nations, and other jurisdictions that have ratified this Constitution.
  • (q) Personal Property means movable property and possessions owned by an individual, excluding land and permanent structures which cannot be owned under Article VII.
  • (r) Lease means a contractual arrangement granting use rights to land for a specified term not exceeding 99 years, as prescribed in Article VII, Section 7.
  • (s) Annual Citizenship Tax means the tax equal to 0.1% of the combined value of a registered individual's total assets and annual income, as prescribed in Article VIII, Section 5.
  • (t) Federal Retirement Account means the investment account established for each registered citizen as prescribed in Article VIII, Section 4.
  • (u) Public Auditor means the elected official responsible for auditing federal government accounts, serving a 2-year term as prescribed in Article XIV, Section 3.
  • (v) Emergency means a situation requiring immediate action due to natural disaster, public health crisis, military threat, civil disturbance, insurrection, or other urgent circumstances that threaten public peace, health, or safety, as determined by the processes prescribed in this Constitution.
  • (w) State means any existing nation-state, sovereign territory, or governmental jurisdiction.
  • (x) Substantially all territory means 95% or more of a territory by both land area and population.
  • (y) Territorial claims means the documented connection of an Indigenous Nation to specific lands, based on continuous presence, cultural practices, stewardship, and recognition by Indigenous Nations with overlapping claims.
  • (z) Constitutional Rights Guardian Panel means the body established under Article XIII, Section 13 to review and certify judicial decisions involving rights enumerated in Article IV and protections established in Article VI.

Section 2.

Terms not defined in this Article shall be interpreted according to their common meaning at the time of interpretation, with consideration for the purposes and principles articulated in Article I.

ARTICLE III - Supremacy

Section 1.

This constitution is the expression of the Sovereignty of the People and is the Supreme Law and Societal Contract upon which all other Societal Intercourse relies, within the Legal Jurisdiction of this constitution.

Section 2.

No Corporation, Nation, or Group may provide financial resources to a candidate for political office, an affiliate to the candidate's election campaign, a family member, an investment, or any other financial connection of the candidate.

Section 3.

Any law, regulation, or governmental action that conflicts with this Constitution is invalid to the extent of such conflict. Citizens may challenge the constitutionality of any measure through the processes established in this Constitution.

ARTICLE IV - Declaration of Rights

Section 1.

No law may deny or impair Freedom of Expression, Peaceable Assembly, Association, or Petition.

Section 2.

No law may be passed establishing a Federal religion, Regional religion, or Municipal religion, or impairing the free exercise of religion, or lack thereof.

Section 3.

No person or Protected Natural Entity may be deprived of life in any case. No person may be deprived of Personal Property in any case, or be denied liberty without due process of law. No person or Protected Natural Entity may be denied the equal protection of the laws.

Section 4.

Equal protection of the laws may not be denied or impaired on account of sex, race, ancestry, country of origin, language, gender, caste, ethnicity, or any other inherent category.

Section 5.

The right of the people to be secure in their persons, homes, documents, actions, thoughts and other possessions against unreasonable search, seizure, or invasion of privacy may not be violated without a warrant. A warrant may not issue except on probable cause, supported by affidavit particularly describing the place to be searched and the persons or things to be seized. Civil asset forfeiture must be followed by a speedy judicial decision whether to return the assets.

Section 6.

The defendant in a case has a right to a speedy public trial, to be informed of the nature of the accusation, to have counsel for defense, to be confronted with the witnesses against the defendant, and to compel attendance of witnesses on the defendant's behalf.

Section 7.

A person may not be compelled to give evidence that may be used against him in a criminal case, or be twice put in jeopardy for the same offense.

Section 8.

Excessive and disproportionate bail may not be required, excessive or disproportionate fines imposed, or cruel and/or unusual punishments inflicted. The writ of habeas corpus may not be suspended in any case.

Section 9.

Capital punishment, involuntary servitude, imprisonment for debt, and slavery are prohibited in all cases.

Section 10.

A bill of attainder or ex post facto law may not be passed.

Section 11.

All persons may freely travel and migrate within and throughout the legal jurisdiction of this constitution. No law may establish unreasonable procedures for entry and residence or impose unreasonable barriers to movement based on citizenship status, nationality, or any other protected category under this Constitution.

Section 12.

All citizens shall have a right to reliable internet access, which shall be provided as a public utility necessary for participation in federal governance. Offline alternatives for all governance functions shall be maintained as prescribed in Article XI, Section 13.

Section 13.

Protected Natural Entities shall have the right to exist, flourish, regenerate, and maintain their ecological integrity. These rights shall be enforced through guardians appointed as prescribed in Article VI, Section 2. The specific protections, stewardship obligations, and enforcement mechanisms for Protected Natural Entities are further prescribed in Article VI.

ARTICLE V - Traditional Rights

Section 1.

Nothing in this Constitution takes away a role or function of a Traditional Leader as recognized by custom and tradition, or prevents a Traditional Leader from being recognized, honored, and given formal or functional roles at any level of government as may be prescribed by this constitution or by statute.

Section 2.

The traditions of the People may be protected by law enacted through Federal Vote. If challenged as violative of Article IV, protection of tradition shall be considered a compelling social purpose warranting such governmental action.

ARTICLE VI - Ecological Stewardship

Section 1.

Right to a healthy environment:

  • (a) All persons have the right to a clean, healthy, and sustainable environment. This right encompasses clean air, clean water, healthy soils, a stable climate, and thriving biodiversity.
  • (b) This right is enforceable and may be invoked by any citizen, Indigenous Nation, or guardian of a Protected Natural Entity in any court of competent jurisdiction.

Section 2.

Protected Natural Entities:

  • (a) Rivers, lakes, aquifers, wetlands, shorelines, coral reefs, glaciers, mountains, tundras, jungles, forests, deserts, and other natural features of ecological significance may be designated as Protected Natural Entities by Regional, Indigenous Nation, or Federal law.
  • (b) Protected Natural Entities possess the right to exist, flourish, regenerate, and maintain their ecological integrity.
  • (c) Protected Natural Entities shall have legal standing to enforce their rights through appointed guardians.
  • (d) Guardianship of Protected Natural Entities shall be assigned primarily to the appropriate Indigenous Nations with historical and ancestral connections to the entity, in recognition of their traditional stewardship roles. Where no appropriate Indigenous Nation exists or where an Indigenous Nation declines guardianship, guardianship may be assigned by Regional or Federal law.
  • (e) Guardians shall have standing to bring legal action on behalf of Protected Natural Entities in any court of competent jurisdiction.
  • (f) The process for designating new Protected Natural Entities and appointing guardians shall be prescribed by law.

Section 3.

Environmental impact assessment:

  • (a) No major land use decision, resource extraction, infrastructure project, or industrial activity may proceed without a comprehensive environmental impact assessment.
  • (b) Environmental impact assessments shall evaluate potential effects on air, water, soil, biodiversity, climate, and the ecological integrity of any Protected Natural Entities within the affected area.
  • (c) Environmental impact assessments shall be conducted by independent bodies and shall be made publicly available.
  • (d) Projects found to pose significant environmental harm may only proceed if approved by the appropriate Indigenous Nations and by a two-thirds majority of citizens in the affected Region.

Section 4.

Climate and emissions:

  • (a) The Federal government shall establish and maintain binding targets for the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions, consistent with the best available scientific evidence and the goal of preventing catastrophic climate change.
  • (b) Emissions reduction targets shall be reviewed and updated at least every 5 years through Federal Vote.
  • (c) All levels of government shall integrate climate adaptation measures into their planning, infrastructure, and governance processes.
  • (d) The Federal government shall provide resources and support to Regions and Indigenous Nations for climate adaptation, with priority given to communities most vulnerable to climate impacts.

Section 5.

Biodiversity:

  • (a) The Federal government, in partnership with Regions and Indigenous Nations, shall take all necessary measures to protect and restore biodiversity within the Legal Jurisdiction of this constitution.
  • (b) The introduction, testing, or release of invasive species or genetically modified organisms into natural ecosystems requires approval by Federal Vote and the consent of appropriate Indigenous Nations.
  • (c) Indigenous Nations' traditional ecological knowledge shall be recognized as a valid and valuable source of information for biodiversity conservation and management.

Section 6.

Prohibition on irreversible ecological harm:

  • (a) No governmental action, private enterprise, or individual activity may cause irreversible destruction of a natural ecosystem, extinction of a species, or permanent contamination of waterways or aquifers within the Legal Jurisdiction of this constitution.
  • (b) Actions that risk irreversible ecological harm may only proceed if approved by a three-fourths majority in Federal Vote and a simple majority of all Indigenous Nations party to this constitution.
  • (c) The precautionary principle shall guide all environmental decision-making: where scientific evidence suggests potential irreversible harm, the burden of proof lies with those proposing the action to demonstrate safety, not with those seeking to prevent the harm.

Section 7.

Indigenous Nations as ecological stewards:

  • (a) Indigenous Nations are recognized as primary stewards of the ecological health of their territories, in keeping with their traditions of environmental guardianship.
  • (b) Indigenous ecological knowledge, practices, and management systems shall be given equal standing with scientific methodologies in environmental assessment and decision-making.
  • (c) Federal and Regional environmental policies shall be developed in meaningful consultation with appropriate Indigenous Nations.
  • (d) Indigenous Nations shall have the right to monitor environmental conditions within their territories and to bring enforcement actions for violations of this Article.

Section 8.

Intergenerational trust:

  • (a) The land, waters, air, and living systems within the Legal Jurisdiction of this constitution are held in trust for present and future generations.
  • (b) All environmental laws, policies, and decisions shall consider their impact on future generations extending at least seven generations into the future.
  • (c) Anything that may compromise the ability of future generations to meet their ecological needs is prohibited under this Constitution.

Section 9.

Enforcement:

  • (a) Any citizen, Indigenous Nation, or guardian of a Protected Natural Entity may bring legal action to enforce the provisions of this Article.
  • (b) Violations of this Article shall be subject to penalties prescribed by law, including restoration requirements, financial penalties, and criminal liability for knowing or willful violations.
  • (c) The Federal government shall establish an environmental monitoring and enforcement agency, subject to the rotating oversight provisions of Article XII, Section 9.

ARTICLE VII - Territory

Section 1.

The territory is organized into Regions which are composed of neighborhoods and municipalities. All such constituent jurisdictions form the Region; Regions may not contain enclaves or exclaves.

Section 2.

The territory includes that of the Regions that ratify this constitution. Unless limited by international treaty obligations assumed by this union, or by its own act, the waters connecting the islands of an archipelago are internal waters regardless of dimensions, and jurisdiction extends to a marine space of 200 miles measured outward from appropriate baselines, the seabed, subsoil, water column, insular or continental shelves. The territory additionally includes airspace over land or water, and any other territory or waters belonging to a region by historic right, custom, or legal title, provided it is not violative of Article VII, Section 1.

Section 3.

The territory of each Region is comprised of the territory of its constituent jurisdictions. A marine boundary between adjacent Regions is determined by law, applying the principle of equidistance.

Section 4.

Territory may be added to the union by vote of the inhabitants of the area, if any, and by Federal Vote.

Section 5.

New Regions may be formed and admitted by law, subject to the same rights, duties, and obligations as provided for in this constitution.

Section 6.

No territory in the form of land, water, air or any other form may be owned, sold, or held in perpetuity by any individual, group, company, corporation, or any other entity. Land is owned jointly by Regional governments and the appropriate Indigenous Nations, and in rare cases at the Federal level through lease from Regions and Indigenous Nations.

Section 7.

Land use and leasing:

  • (a) All land within a Region is owned jointly by that Region and the Indigenous Nations with recognized territorial claims to that land. The determination of which Indigenous Nations have territorial claims shall be based on documented historical or ancestral connection, continuous presence, cultural practices, stewardship, and recognition by appropriate Indigenous Nations.
  • (b) Regions and Indigenous Nations may jointly lease land to individuals, corporations, the federal government, or other entities for terms not to exceed 99 years.
  • (c) Leases may be renewed upon expiration according to terms prescribed by Regional or Indigenous Nation law. Automatic renewal may be included as a provision in the original lease agreement.
  • (d) Leasehold interests may be inherited, sold, or transferred according to the terms of the lease and applicable Regional or Indigenous Nation law.
  • (e) The terms, conditions, costs, and procedures for obtaining and maintaining land leases shall be determined by each Region and appropriate Indigenous Nations, having due regard for ensuring access to land for all citizens.
  • (f) Use rights, including the right to build, modify, sublease, or otherwise utilize leased land, shall be determined by Regional and Indigenous Nation law and specified in lease agreements.
  • (g) Conditions under which a lease may be terminated prior to expiration shall be determined by Regional and Indigenous Nation law, with such terminations subject to fair compensation and due process as prescribed by this Constitution.
  • (h) Distinctions between residential, commercial, agricultural, and other categories of land use may be established by Regional and Indigenous Nation law.
  • (i) Major land use decisions, including those affecting parcels larger than the average lot size in the Region or involving resource extraction, require approval from the appropriate Indigenous Nations and shall be subject to environmental impact assessment as prescribed in Article VI, Section 3. The process for such approval, including caucusing, voting thresholds, and timelines, shall be determined by the Indigenous Nations themselves through mechanisms established by law.
  • (j) In cases of disagreement between a Region and appropriate Indigenous Nations regarding land use, the matter may be resolved through the Federal judicial process as prescribed in Article XIII, or through alternative dispute resolution mechanisms established by the parties.

Section 8.

Voluntary withdrawal from the union:

  • (a) This union is voluntary. Any Region or Indigenous Nation may withdraw from this union through the process prescribed in this Section.
  • (b) A withdrawal referendum shall be initiated by petition of at least 25% of the registered citizens of the withdrawing jurisdiction, or by a resolution of the Indigenous Nation's governing body through its own processes.
  • (c) Upon receipt of a valid petition or resolution, a withdrawal referendum shall be held within the withdrawing jurisdiction no sooner than 6 months and no later than 12 months after the petition is validated. This waiting period ensures deliberation and cannot be waived.
  • (d) Withdrawal requires approval by a three-fourths majority of registered citizens in the withdrawing jurisdiction who cast a vote, with a minimum participation rate of 80% of registered citizens.
  • (e) If a withdrawal referendum fails to achieve the required threshold, a new withdrawal petition for the same jurisdiction may not be submitted for at least 5 years.
  • (f) Upon successful withdrawal:
    • (i) A transition period of no less than 2 years shall commence, during which the withdrawing jurisdiction and the Federal government shall negotiate the orderly transfer of obligations, assets, and liabilities;
    • (ii) The withdrawing jurisdiction's share of Federal debts, treaty obligations, and ongoing commitments shall be determined by Federal Vote;
    • (iii) Citizens of the withdrawing jurisdiction shall retain citizenship in this union until the conclusion of the transition period, at which point they must choose between citizenship in this union (by re-registering in a remaining jurisdiction) or citizenship in the withdrawing jurisdiction;
    • (iv) Land within the withdrawing jurisdiction remains subject to the joint ownership provisions of this Article until the transition is complete, after which the withdrawing jurisdiction and the appropriate Indigenous Nations shall determine land governance through their own processes;
    • (v) Indigenous Nations with territorial claims within a withdrawing Region retain all rights under this Constitution if they choose to remain party to this union, regardless of the Region's withdrawal.
  • (g) A jurisdiction that has withdrawn may petition to rejoin the union through the ratification process prescribed in Article XIX.
  • (h) When an Indigenous Nation withdraws from the union while the Region or Regions encompassing its territorial claims remain:
    • (i) Joint ownership of land within the withdrawing Nation's recognized territorial claims shall be restructured. The withdrawing Nation's ownership interest shall be redistributed among the remaining Indigenous Nations with recognized overlapping or adjacent claims to that territory, as determined by those Nations through their own processes. Where no other Indigenous Nation holds recognized claims, the ownership interest shall be held in trust by the Federal government until such claims are established or until the withdrawing Nation rejoins the union;
    • (ii) Guardianship of any Protected Natural Entities assigned to the withdrawing Nation under Article VI shall be reassigned to other appropriate Indigenous Nations with connections to the entity, or to Regional or Federal guardianship as prescribed in Article VI, Section 2(d);
    • (iii) The withdrawing Nation's share of revenue from land leases and resource activities under Article XI, Section 11(c) shall be redistributed among the remaining Indigenous Nations with recognized claims to the relevant territory, or held in the Federal Treasury if no such Nations exist;
    • (iv) Citizens registered with the withdrawing Indigenous Nation shall have the same rights and transition period prescribed in subsection (f)(iii) of this Section;
    • (v) The withdrawing Indigenous Nation retains the right to negotiate bilateral agreements with Regions and remaining Indigenous Nations regarding continued access to sacred sites, traditional gathering areas, and culturally significant locations within their former territorial claims;
    • (vi) Withdrawal by an Indigenous Nation does not extinguish that Nation's historical and ancestral connection to its territories. If the Nation subsequently rejoins the union under Article XIX, its territorial claims shall be restored to the extent they have not been superseded by new claims recognized during the period of withdrawal.

ARTICLE VIII - Citizenship

Section 1.

Citizenship is conferred to all registered individuals who have paid or had deducted the Annual Citizenship Tax for the relevant fiscal year.

Section 2.

A person 18 years or older registers as a citizen by submitting their name and Region ID to the Citizen Registry. Registering as a citizen of more than 1 Region, Municipality, Neighborhood, or Indigenous Nation is prohibited.

Section 3.

A child of a citizen may be registered in the Citizen Registry by their citizen parent or citizen legal guardian. Fingerprints and/or a birth certificate may be submitted in lieu of the required Region ID.

Section 4.

Federal Retirement Account:

  • (a) Upon registration in the Citizen Registry, a Federal Retirement Account shall be established for each registered individual.
  • (b) Federal Retirement Accounts shall be managed by a federal financial agency or through a decentralized system as prescribed by law.
  • (c) Funds in Federal Retirement Accounts shall be invested in securities and financial markets to generate growth over time, with investment strategies prescribed by law.
  • (d) Account holders may not withdraw funds from their Federal Retirement Account until reaching 65 years of age, except as provided in this section.
  • (e) Account holders may borrow up to 50% of their account's current value for any purpose. Such loans shall be subject to terms prescribed by law and shall remain valid obligations even if the borrower renounces citizenship.
  • (f) Upon an account holder's death, the account value shall be transferred to designated inheritor(s) according to the account holder's specifications:
    • Each inheritor shall receive either a specified value or a specified percentage of the total account value at time of death;
    • If the sum of specified values exceeds the total account value, each inheritor shall receive a proportional share calculated by dividing their specified value by the sum of all specified values, then multiplying by the actual account value;
    • If no inheritors are designated, the account value shall be transferred to the general fund.
  • (g) Account holders who renounce citizenship under this Constitution forfeit all claims to their Federal Retirement Account, and no inheritors shall receive any portion thereof.
  • (h) Any registered citizen may elect to forgo their Federal Retirement Account by submitting written notice to the Federal Revenue Agency. Such election:
    • Must be made voluntarily without coercion;
    • Results in forfeiture of any accumulated account value to the general fund;
    • Does not relieve the individual of responsibility for the Annual Citizenship Tax;
    • Does not affect the individual's citizenship status or voting rights.

Section 5.

Annual Citizenship Tax:

  • (a) The Annual Citizenship Tax shall be 0.1% of the combined value of a registered individual's total assets and annual income.
  • (b) The tax is due annually on January 1st, with a grace period extending to June 30th of the same year.
  • (c) Citizens may pay the Annual Citizenship Tax up to one year in advance.
  • (d) If a registered individual is unable to pay the Annual Citizenship Tax by the end of the grace period, the tax amount shall be automatically deducted from their Federal Retirement Account.
  • (e) Revenue from the Annual Citizenship Tax shall be deposited into the general fund of the Federal Treasury.
  • (f) Upon paying or having deducted the Annual Citizenship Tax, a registered individual is confirmed as a citizen for the relevant fiscal year.

Section 6.

Asset and Income Reporting:

  • (a) All registered individuals shall report their total assets and annual income to the Federal Revenue Agency by January 1st of each year for purposes of calculating the Annual Citizenship Tax.
  • (b) Assets subject to reporting include, but are not limited to: financial accounts, investments, vehicles, valuables, business interests, and any other items of significant monetary value. Leasehold interests in land or buildings are not considered assets for this purpose.
  • (c) Aggregate asset and income data shall be made publicly available for purposes of tracking wealth distribution and economic inequality.
  • (d) Individual asset and income data shall remain confidential except as required for tax administration and law enforcement purposes.
  • (e) An appeals process shall be established by law to address disputes regarding asset reporting, valuations, or tax calculations. The grounds for appeal shall not be limited to specific circumstances.
  • (f) Individuals who fail to report assets and income may do so at any time thereafter and shall have their citizenship confirmed upon reporting and paying or having deducted any outstanding Annual Citizenship Tax.
  • (g) Intentional fraud in asset or income reporting shall be subject to criminal penalties as prescribed by law.

Section 7.

Registration of Adults:

  • (a) Any person 18 years or older who is not currently a registered citizen may register by submitting their name and Region ID to the Citizen Registry and paying the Annual Citizenship Tax for the current fiscal year.
  • (b) Upon registration and payment or deduction of the Annual Citizenship Tax, a Federal Retirement Account shall be established for the newly registered citizen.

Section 8.

Rights of Registered Children:

  • (a) Upon a registered child's 18th birthday, the individual may choose to renounce citizenship under this Constitution, in which case their Federal Retirement Account shall be forfeited to the general fund, or the individual may choose to assume responsibility for their own Annual Citizenship Tax.
  • (b) Children who choose to maintain citizenship shall make their first independent Annual Citizenship Tax payment or have it deducted from their Federal Retirement Account by their 19th birthday.

Section 9.

Incentivized Participation:

  • (a) Citizens who participate in federal voting beyond the minimum required percentage as prescribed in Article XI shall receive small contributions to their Federal Retirement Accounts as an incentive for civic engagement.
  • (b) The amount of such contributions shall be prescribed by law and shall be modest so as not to create undue financial incentives for voting patterns.

Section 10.

Rights and status of non-citizens:

  • (a) Nothing in this Constitution requires any person to become a citizen. Persons may reside within Regional territories subject to the laws of that Region and appropriate Indigenous Nations, while choosing not to participate in citizenship.
  • (b) Non-citizens residing within Regional territories retain all rights enumerated in Article IV (Declaration of Rights) except those specifically reserved for citizens by this Constitution.
  • (c) Non-citizens may apply to lease land subject to the restrictions in Article XV, Sections 4 and 5, and as permitted by Regional or Indigenous Nation law.

ARTICLE IX - Levels of Government

Section 1.

The four levels of government under this constitution are Federal, Regional, Municipal, and Local. Each shall have a similarly open source constitution. All such constitutions are invalid to the extent that they conflict with this constitution.

Section 2.

The Local level of government consists of neighborhoods with a population of no less than 150 Individuals, or 10 separate, permanently occupied dwellings, and not exceeding 1,000 Individuals or 500 separate, permanently occupied dwellings.

Section 3.

The Municipal level of government consists of two or more neighborhoods that agree to form a municipality.

Section 4.

The Regional level of government consists of two or more municipalities that agree to form a region. For the purposes of Federal Governance, regions shall not contain other regions as geographic enclaves or exclaves.

Section 5.

The Federal level of government consists of all Indigenous Nations and Regions that ratify this constitution.

ARTICLE X - Powers of Government

Section 1.

A power expressly delegated to the Federal government, or a power of such an indisputably Federal character as to be beyond the power of a Region to control, is a Federal power.

Section 2.

A power not expressly delegated to the Federal government or prohibited to the Region is a Regional power.

Section 3.

Regional, Municipal and Local governments are prohibited from imposing taxes which restrict interjurisdictional commerce.

Section 4.

Indigenous Nations possess sovereign powers equivalent to Regional powers, including the authority to govern their peoples, manage jointly-owned territories in accordance with Article VII, establish judicial systems, and exercise all powers not expressly delegated to the Federal government or prohibited by this Constitution.

ARTICLE XI - Legislative

Section 1.

The legislative power of the government is vested in the People, who exercise this power through direct participation via secure digital platforms and offline voting facilities, and through mandatory voting on all proposed federal measures.

Section 2.

The four levels of government under this Constitution shall each have legislative systems outlined in their Constitutions. All such systems are invalid to the extent that they conflict with this Constitution.

Section 3.

Federal legislation operates through a continuous collaborative process:

  • (a) Proposal Platform: A secure online collaborative deliberative platform enables any citizen to propose new laws, amendments, or policy initiatives. This platform operates similarly to structured argument mapping systems, allowing:
    • hierarchical organization of proposals with supporting and opposing arguments;
    • collaborative refinement of legal language by multiple citizens;
    • nested commentary and discussion on specific provisions;
    • evidence linking and citation requirements;
    • version tracking of all edits and contributions.
  • (b) Quarterly Cycle: Proposals advance through a four-phase quarterly cycle:
    • Phase 1 (Months 1-3): Drafting period during which proposals are submitted, refined through collaborative editing, and discussed publicly;
    • Phase 2 (Months 4-6): Voting period during which all registered citizens vote on proposals submitted in the previous drafting period;
    • Phase 3 (Months 7-9): Drafting period for new proposals;
    • Phase 4 (Months 10-12): Voting period for proposals submitted in Phase 3.
  • (c) Continuous Ballots: Proposals not withdrawn by their proponents remain on subsequent ballots until ratified or withdrawn, allowing measures to build support over multiple cycles.
  • (d) Enactment: Proposals receiving a simple majority of votes cast are enacted into law at the conclusion of the voting period.

Section 4.

Platform security and integrity:

  • (a) The collaborative legislative platform and all voting systems shall employ security measures prescribed by law to prevent fraud, manipulation, and unauthorized access;
  • (b) Security protocols shall include identity verification, encryption, comprehensive audit trails, and mechanisms for detecting irregularities;
  • (c) All security measures and their implementation shall be publicly documented and subject to independent audit by international observers;
  • (d) Regular security assessments and updates shall be mandatory, with results reported to all citizens.

Section 5.

Voting requirements and participation:

  • (a) All registered citizens are required to cast votes, though blank votes are permitted;
  • (b) Citizens must vote on a minimum of 10% of federal measures on each ballot, focusing on matters of federal significance;
  • (c) Regional, Municipal, and Local measures are subject to similar requirements at their respective levels as prescribed by their constitutions;
  • (d) Additional voluntary participation beyond the minimum requirement is incentivized through small contributions to the citizen's federal retirement account;
  • (e) The collaborative legislative platform shall provide educational materials, summaries, and deliberative discussions for each measure to facilitate informed participation.

Section 6.

Indigenous Nations legislative participation:

  • (a) All proposed legislation shall be made available to all Indigenous Nations party to this constitution for review during the voting period;
  • (b) Any Indigenous Nation may choose to participate in voting on any proposed measure. If at least one Indigenous Nation chooses to participate in voting on a measure, all Indigenous Nations shall have the opportunity to vote on that measure;
  • (c) When Indigenous Nations participate in voting on a measure, the measure is enacted only if it receives:
    • A simple majority of citizens voting in favor; AND
    • A simple majority of Indigenous Nations voting in favor;
  • (d) If no Indigenous Nations choose to participate in voting on a measure, it is enacted if it receives a simple majority of citizens voting in favor;
  • (e) Indigenous Nations determine their own internal processes for deciding how to vote on federal measures.
  • (f) The mechanisms by which Indigenous Nations coordinate their votes on federal measures, including timelines, caucusing procedures, and internal decision-making processes, will be determined by the Nations themselves through collaborative processes. This Constitution intentionally defers to Indigenous self-determination on these matters.

Section 7.

Emergency legislative procedures:

  • (a) Measures requiring immediate action due to natural disaster, public health emergency, military threat, or other urgent circumstances may be designated for emergency voting;
  • (b) Emergency measures follow an accelerated cycle lasting no more than seven days from proposal to vote;
  • (c) Emergency designation requires approval by a two-thirds vote of citizens voting on the emergency designation;
  • (d) All emergency measures enacted shall be subject to review and potential reversal in the next regular voting cycle.

Section 8.

Constitutional amendments:

  • (a) Amendments to this Constitution may be proposed through the same collaborative platform and quarterly cycle as ordinary legislation;
  • (b) Constitutional amendments require a simple majority of votes cast to be ratified, except for amendments to Article IV (Declaration of Rights) and Article VI (Ecological Stewardship), which require a three-fourths majority;
  • (c) Regional constitutional amendments follow similar procedures at the Regional level with thresholds prescribed by Regional constitutions.

Section 9.

Federal legislative powers - The following legislative powers and obligations are expressly delegated to the Federal government:

  • (a) to provide for Federal defense;
  • (b) to ratify Federal treaties;
  • (c) to regulate citizenship as it pertains to this union;
  • (d) to impose taxes, duties, and tariffs based on imports;
  • (e) to impose taxes on income and on citizenship to this union;
  • (f) to issue and/or regulate currency;
  • (g) to regulate banking, foreign and interjurisdictional commerce, insurance, the issuance and use of commercial paper and securities, bankruptcy and insolvency, and patents and copyrights;
  • (h) to regulate navigation and shipping, except within bodies of water entirely within the jurisdiction of a single Region or Indigenous Nation;
  • (i) to establish usury limits on major loans;
  • (j) to provide for a Federal postal system;
  • (k) to establish and regulate a Federal public service system;
  • (l) to define Federal crimes and prescribe penalties, having due regard for local custom and tradition;
  • (m) to promote education and health by setting minimum standards, coordinating Federal activities relating to foreign assistance, providing training and assistance to Regions and Indigenous Nations and providing support for postsecondary educational programs and projects;
  • (n) to establish and enforce environmental protections, emissions standards, and ecological stewardship obligations as prescribed in Article VI.

Section 10.

Concurrent powers - The following powers may be exercised concurrently by the Federal government and lower levels of government:

  • (a) to appropriate public funds;
  • (b) to borrow money on the public credit;
  • (c) to establish systems of social security and public welfare;
  • (d) to regulate navigation and shipping in bodies of water entirely within the jurisdiction of a single Region or Indigenous Nation.

Section 11.

Tax uniformity and distribution:

  • (a) Taxes shall be imposed uniformly;
  • (b) Not less than 75% of revenues shall be paid into the treasury of the jurisdiction from which the taxes are collected;
  • (c) Net revenue derived from all land leases, resource exploitation, and related activities under the joint ownership of Regions and Indigenous Nations shall be divided such that:
    • The appropriate Indigenous Nations collectively receive 50% of the net revenue, to be divided equally among all Indigenous Nations with recognized claims to the territory;
    • The relevant Region receives 25% of the net revenue;
    • The Federal government receives 25% of the net revenue.
  • (d) Revenue from navigation and shipping activities prescribed under Section 9(h) and Section 10(d) shall be distributed according to the same formula in subsection (c).

Section 12.

Public service compensation: Anyone employed under provisions outlined in this constitution may receive an annual salary and allowances totaling no more than twice the median annual salary of the Region in which they are registered as a citizen, estimated for the most recent relevant fiscal year.

Section 13.

Offline and alternative voting:

  • (a) Offline voting facilities shall be maintained at all times as a permanent parallel system to the digital voting platform, ensuring that no citizen is excluded from participation due to digital infrastructure failures, personal circumstances, or preference.
  • (b) Each Neighborhood shall maintain at least one physical voting location accessible to all citizens, staffed during all voting periods and available for emergency votes.
  • (c) Offline voting methods shall include paper ballots with secure handling, counting, and auditing procedures prescribed by law, including but not limited to: multiple witnesses at each stage of ballot handling, sealed ballot containers with tamper-evident mechanisms, documented chain of custody from casting through final count, and the right of any citizen to observe ballot handling and counting processes.
  • (d) Offline votes shall carry equal weight to digital votes and shall be integrated into final tallies through secure, auditable processes.
  • (e) In any discrepancy between digital vote tallies and analog paper ballot records, the analog paper ballots shall be treated as the authoritative record, subject to verification procedures prescribed by law.
  • (f) In the event of widespread digital infrastructure failure during a voting period:
    • (i) The voting period shall be automatically extended by a duration equal to the period of disruption, up to a maximum extension of 30 days;
    • (ii) Offline voting facilities shall scale operations to accommodate increased demand;
    • (iii) If digital infrastructure cannot be restored within 30 days, the vote shall be completed entirely through offline means;
    • (iv) Emergency votes under Section 7 of this Article shall default to offline procedures if digital systems are compromised.
  • (g) The Federal government shall maintain and regularly test contingency plans for governance continuity during prolonged digital infrastructure disruptions.
  • (h) Accessibility and alternative participation:
    • (i) Accessibility accommodations for citizens with disabilities shall be provided at all physical voting locations, as prescribed by law;
    • (ii) Regional governments shall provide additional participation methods including but not limited to: telephone voting systems, mobile voting units serving remote or underserved areas, and in-person assistance centers for citizens with limited digital literacy;
    • (iii) No citizen shall be penalized or disadvantaged for choosing to participate through offline or alternative methods rather than digital platforms.
  • (i) The collaborative legislative platform prescribed in Section 3 shall maintain offline equivalents, including physical community bulletin boards, printed proposal summaries distributed to Neighborhood voting locations, and in-person deliberation forums, to ensure that citizens without digital access may participate in the drafting and deliberation process.

Section 14.

Minimum participation for enactment:

  • (a) No federal measure shall be enacted unless at least 75% of all registered citizens have cast a vote on that specific measure. Measures that receive a majority of votes cast and receive votes from at least 50% of all registered citizens, but fail to meet the 75% threshold shall be featured prominently in citizen educational materials during the next voting period.
  • (b) Constitutional amendments require that at least 80% of all registered citizens have cast a vote on the amendment.
  • (c) Emergency measures under Section 7 of this Article are exempt from these thresholds, but shall be subject to review under standard thresholds in the next regular voting cycle.
  • (d) These thresholds may be adjusted by Federal Vote, provided that the threshold for ordinary legislation shall not fall below 67% or exceed 90%, and the threshold for constitutional amendments shall not fall below 75% or exceed 95%.

Section 15.

Vote delegation:

  • (a) Any citizen may voluntarily delegate their vote on specific measures or categories of measures to another citizen, referred to as a delegate. Delegation shall be:
    • (i) Specific to identified measures, categories, or policy areas, and not a blanket transfer of voting authority;
    • (ii) Revocable at any time prior to the close of the voting period for any given measure;
    • (iii) Transparent the total number of delegated votes held by any delegate shall be publicly visible, though the identity of delegating citizens shall remain confidential;
    • (iv) Limited no single delegate may cast more than a number of delegated votes equal to 1% of the registered citizens in the delegate's Region on any single measure, to prevent excessive concentration of voting power.
  • (b) A delegated vote counts toward the delegating citizen's minimum voting requirement under Section 5 of this Article.
  • (c) No citizen may be compelled, coerced, or financially incentivized to delegate their vote. Offering or accepting payment or other consideration in exchange for vote delegation shall be subject to criminal penalties prescribed by law.
  • (d) Delegates shall not receive any compensation, contribution to their Federal Retirement Account, or other benefit for votes cast on behalf of others.
  • (e) If a citizen has delegated their vote on a measure but subsequently casts a direct vote on the same measure before the close of voting, the direct vote shall supersede the delegation for that measure.
  • (f) The delegation system shall be implemented through the secure digital platform with the same security protections prescribed in Section 4 of this Article, and through offline equivalents prescribed in Section 13.
  • (g) Indigenous Nations may establish their own delegation mechanisms consistent with the principles of this Section and with respect for each Nation's traditions of collective decision-making. Such mechanisms:
    • (i) Shall be determined by each Indigenous Nation through its own governance processes;
    • (ii) May differ from the delegation mechanisms available to citizens of Regions, provided they maintain transparency, voluntariness, and protection against coercion;
    • (iii) Shall not affect the separate vote of the Indigenous Nation itself under Section 6 of this Article, which remains a collective decision of the Nation as a whole.

Section 16.

Measure categorization and citizen guidance:

  • (a) All proposed measures submitted for voting shall be categorized by subject area, including but not limited to: defense and foreign affairs, ecological stewardship, economic regulation, education and health, infrastructure, justice and rights, and Indigenous affairs.
  • (b) The collaborative legislative platform shall provide for each measure:
    • (i) A plain-language summary of no more than 500 words;
    • (ii) A structured presentation of the principal arguments for and against the measure, contributed collaboratively during the drafting period;
    • (iii) An assessment of the measure's projected fiscal impact;
    • (iv) Identification of any Article IV or Article VI rights implicated by the measure.
  • (c) Citizens may register interest in specific subject categories and receive prioritized notifications when measures in those categories are submitted for voting.
  • (d) The educational materials and summaries prescribed in this Section shall be made available through offline equivalents as prescribed in Section 13.

ARTICLE XII - Executive

Section 1.

The executive power of the government is vested in the People, who exercise this power through direct participation in governance via secure digital platforms and offline facilities.

Section 2.

The day-to-day administration of federal governance operates through online systems modeled on collaborative deliberative platforms. These systems enable:

  • (a) citizen proposals of laws, policies, and initiatives;
  • (b) collaborative refinement of proposals through structured argument mapping and discussion;
  • (c) public review and commentary on all proposed measures;
  • (d) mandatory voting on federal measures by all registered citizens;
  • (e) implementation of ratified measures by rotating citizen committees.
  • (f) analog backup systems for emergency governance coordination, including:
    • (i) designated physical meeting spaces at each governmental level, maintained in a state of readiness;
    • (ii) radio and broadcast communication networks for official government communications;
    • (iii) courier and postal notification systems for distributing official notices, proposals, and emergency information;
    • (iv) public bulletin boards for official notices maintained in accessible locations within each Neighborhood.

Section 3.

Laws and policies are proposed and ratified through the continuous quarterly cycle prescribed in Article XI, with citizens required to vote on a minimum percentage of federal measures and additional participation incentivized through small contributions to their federal retirement accounts.

Section 4.

Emergency measures requiring immediate action may be subject to a special vote cycle lasting no more than seven days. Such measures may address:

  • (a) natural disasters requiring immediate federal response;
  • (b) threats to public health or safety;
  • (c) imminent military threats or international crises;
  • (d) interjurisdictional emergencies requiring federal coordination;
  • (e) ecological emergencies threatening irreversible environmental harm.

Section 5.

Implementation of enacted laws shall be carried out by:

  • (a) rotating citizen committees with term limits of 2 years, staffed by volunteers or citizens selected by lottery from relevant professional or regional pools. Committee members may not serve consecutive terms, but may serve non-consecutive terms;
  • (b) Regional and Municipal governments within their respective jurisdictions as prescribed by law;
  • (c) federal service agencies managed by rotating oversight councils.

Section 6.

A Council of Representatives may convene and wield executive power if required to preserve public peace, health, or safety, at a time of extreme emergency caused by civil disturbance, natural disaster, immediate threat of war, insurrection, or interjurisdictional emergency.

  • (a) A Council of Representatives is valid only when two-thirds of all constituent jurisdictions are represented;
  • (b) No such council may wield executive power for more than 30 days;
  • (c) A new council of different representatives may convene and be legally valid for another 30 days. Representatives to a Council may not serve on consecutive Councils, but may serve on non-consecutive Councils;
  • (d) Such councils may by statute establish temporary executive departments, which remain valid only while that council is valid;
  • (e) Emergency Council authority and limitations:
    • (i) Emergency Councils may take immediate action on matters of life, health, and safety;
    • (ii) All financial commitments, treaties, legal obligations, and other substantive actions with enduring consequences must be approved by emergency Federal Vote as prescribed in Article XI, Section 7 before being executed;
    • (iii) Military actions may only be taken pursuant to a declaration of war or authorization of force previously approved by Federal Vote, with strategic decisions and significant escalations requiring separate approval;
    • (iv) All actions taken during the emergency shall be subject to comprehensive review by Federal Vote within 30 days of the council's dissolution, with each substantive action voted on separately;
    • (v) Actions that do not receive a simple majority in the review vote are nullified and have no legal effect.

Section 7.

International representation shall be conducted by:

  • (a) delegates elected by Federal Vote for specific purposes and limited terms;
  • (b) rotating representatives to international bodies such as the United Nations;
  • (c) professional diplomatic staff serving under term limits prescribed by statute;
  • (d) all treaties subject to ratification as prescribed in this Constitution.

Section 8.

Defense:

  • (a) The federal government shall maintain no permanent standing offensive military force. The deployment of military force beyond the Legal Jurisdiction of this constitution for offensive purposes requires a declaration of war or authorization of force approved by Federal Vote.
  • (b) Defense readiness infrastructure: The federal government shall establish and maintain:
    • (i) A trained civilian defense reserve, composed of volunteers who receive regular training in defensive operations, disaster response, and emergency coordination. Reserve members shall serve terms prescribed by law and shall be subject to the compensation limits prescribed in Article XI, Section 12 during periods of active service;
    • (ii) Pre-positioned defensive equipment and supplies distributed across Regions in coordination with Regional and Indigenous Nation governments;
    • (iii) A standing defense coordination office, staffed by rotating personnel serving terms not exceeding 2 years, responsible for maintaining defense readiness, coordinating with allied nations, monitoring threats, and maintaining communication systems capable of functioning during digital infrastructure failures;
    • (iv) Cybersecurity defense capabilities for the protection of governance infrastructure, critical systems, and the digital platforms prescribed in this Constitution.
  • (c) Rapid defensive response: In the event of an armed attack, invasion, or imminent military threat against the Legal Jurisdiction of this constitution:
    • (i) The defense coordination office may immediately activate the civilian defense reserve for defensive operations within the Legal Jurisdiction, without prior Federal Vote;
    • (ii) Any such activation must be reported to all citizens within 24 hours through all available communication channels, including analog systems;
    • (iii) A Federal Vote to confirm, modify, or terminate the activation shall be initiated within 72 hours of activation, using emergency voting procedures and defaulting to offline voting if digital systems are compromised;
    • (iv) If the Federal Vote does not confirm the activation, defensive operations shall wind down within 48 hours of the vote's conclusion, except to the extent necessary to protect life and evacuate personnel.
  • (d) Mutual defense treaties with allied nations and Indigenous Nations shall be maintained as a complement to domestic defense readiness.
  • (e) Regional, Indigenous Nation, and Municipal governments may establish civilian defense training programs and community emergency response systems as prescribed by their constitutions.
  • (f) The defense coordination office and all defense readiness activities shall be subject to oversight by rotating citizen committees as prescribed in Section 9 of this Article, and to audit by the Public Auditor as prescribed in Article XIV, Section 3.
  • (g) Nothing in this Section authorizes the establishment of a permanent standing military force, military conscription, or the offensive deployment of military assets without authorization by Federal Vote.

Section 9.

Federal agencies required by this Constitution or by law shall operate under direction of rotating citizen oversight committees elected for terms prescribed by statute, with all members subject to the compensation limits prescribed in Article XI, Section 12.

Section 10.

All citizens shall have a right to reliable internet access, which shall be provided as a public utility necessary for participation in federal governance. Offline alternatives for all governance functions shall be maintained as prescribed in Article XI, Section 13.

ARTICLE XIII - Judicial

Section 1.

The judicial power of the government is vested in the People.

Section 2.

Regions and Indigenous Nations shall each have judicial systems outlined in their Constitutions. All such systems are invalid to the extent that they conflict with this Constitution.

Section 3.

The following judicial powers are expressly delegated to the Federal government:

  • (a) to prescribe maximum sentences;
  • (b) to resolve legal disputes between Indigenous Nations and Regional jurisdictions party to this constitution, cases affecting officials of foreign governments, cases in which the Federal government is a party, cases arising under this constitution, Federal law or treaties, in disputes between a Region and a citizen of another Region, between citizens of different Regions, and between a Region or a citizen thereof, and a foreign State, citizen, or subject.

Section 4.

Indigenous Nations party to this constitution are expressly delegated the power to resolve cases appealed in the judicial system of a single Indigenous Nation. In cases appealed between Indigenous Nations, a judicial panel of Indigenous Nations party to this constitution may convene to evaluate the appeal, or if at least one Nation chooses, the case appeal may be subject to Section 3(b). In the latter scenario, a judicial panel of Indigenous Nations party to this constitution may convene prior to the decision to opine on the case.

Section 5.

Regional jurisdictions party to this constitution are expressly delegated the power to resolve cases appealed in the judicial system of a single Region. If a simple majority of citizens of the relevant Region vote to appeal the case to the Federal government, the case appeal may be subject to Section 3(b).

Section 6.

A judicial decision entails a trial during which the plaintiff and the defendant each present their case and take questions from the citizens of the relevant jurisdictions.

Section 7.

A judicial decision in a Region is ratified when a simple majority of the citizens of the relevant jurisdiction and a simple majority of appropriate Indigenous Nations ratify the decision, except that judicial decisions certified as Rights-Protective under Section 13 of this Article require a three-fourths majority of citizens of the relevant jurisdiction and a two-thirds majority of appropriate Indigenous Nations to be overturned.

Section 8.

A judicial decision at the Federal level regarding constitutional matters or major disputes between Regions and Indigenous Nations is ratified when a simple majority of the citizens of the Legal Jurisdiction of this constitution and a simple majority of all Indigenous Nations party to this constitution ratify the decision, except that judicial decisions certified as Rights-Protective under Section 13 of this Article require a three-fourths majority of citizens and a two-thirds majority of all Indigenous Nations party to this constitution to be overturned.

Section 9.

A judicial decision regarding the appeal of a case in the judicial system of a single Indigenous Nation is ratified when a simple majority of all Indigenous Nations party to this constitution ratify the decision, except that judicial decisions certified as Rights-Protective under Section 13 of this Article require a two-thirds majority of all Indigenous Nations party to this constitution to be overturned.

Section 10.

Judicial decisions shall be consistent with this constitution, with traditional customs and practices, and with the social and geographical configuration of the Legal Jurisdiction of this constitution.

Section 11.

Local land use disputes and minor cases:

  • (a) Disputes between a Region and appropriate Indigenous Nations regarding land use for parcels smaller than the average lot size or not involving resource extraction may be resolved through regional judicial processes without requiring Federal Vote.
  • (b) Mechanisms for alternative dispute resolution, mediation, and arbitration between Regions and Indigenous Nations may be established by law.
  • (c) Emergency land use decisions necessitated by natural disasters, public health emergencies, or imminent threats may be made by regional authorities subject to retrospective review by appropriate Indigenous Nations within a reasonable timeframe prescribed by law.

Section 12.

Constitutional challenges:

  • (a) Any citizen may challenge the constitutionality of a law, regulation, or governmental action by submitting a petition through the collaborative legislative platform.
  • (b) Constitutional challenges shall be decided by emergency Federal Vote as prescribed in Article XI, Section 7. During this 7-day period, the challenged measure shall be suspended.
  • (c) The constitutional challenge is decided when:
    • A simple majority of citizens voting find the measure unconstitutional; OR
    • A simple majority of all Indigenous Nations party to this constitution find the measure unconstitutional.
  • (d) Measures found unconstitutional are permanently struck down. Measures not found unconstitutional are reinstated and may not be challenged again for at least 2 years.
  • (e) Decisions on constitutional challenges may be appealed through the same process as other judicial decisions prescribed in this Article.

Section 13.

Constitutional Rights Guardian Panel:

  • (a) A Constitutional Rights Guardian Panel shall be established to review judicial decisions involving rights enumerated in Article IV (Declaration of Rights) and protections established in Article VI (Ecological Stewardship) before such decisions are submitted to popular ratification.
  • (b) The Panel shall be composed of:
    • Three members selected by lottery from among citizens with demonstrated legal expertise, serving staggered terms of 2 years;
    • Three members selected by Indigenous Nations party to this constitution through processes determined by the Nations themselves, serving staggered terms of 2 years;
    • One member selected by lottery from the general citizenry, serving a term of 2 years.
  • (c) Panel members may not serve consecutive terms, but may serve non-consecutive terms. Panel members are subject to the compensation limits prescribed in Article XI, Section 12.
  • (d) Upon receiving a judicial decision involving Article IV or Article VI rights, the Panel shall review the decision and, within 30 days, issue one of the following certifications:
    • (i) Rights-Protective Certification: The decision directly enforces or protects rights enumerated in Article IV or Article VI. Decisions receiving this certification may only be overturned by a three-fourths majority of citizens voting and a two-thirds majority of all Indigenous Nations party to this constitution;
    • (ii) Standard Certification: The decision involves Article IV or Article VI rights but does not directly turn on their enforcement. Such decisions proceed to ratification under the standard thresholds prescribed in this Article.
  • (e) The Panel's certification determines only the voting threshold applicable to the decision; the Panel may not alter, amend, or substitute its judgment for the substance of the judicial decision itself.
  • (f) The Panel's certification may be challenged by any citizen through the collaborative legislative platform. Such challenges shall be decided by emergency Federal Vote as prescribed in Article XI, Section 7, requiring a two-thirds majority to override the Panel's certification.
  • (g) The Panel shall publish written reasoning for each certification, made available to all citizens through the governance platform and offline equivalents.
  • (h) Panel members may be removed for cause by Federal Vote requiring a two-thirds majority. Vacancies shall be filled through the same selection mechanism as the original appointment.

ARTICLE XIV - Financial & Structural Integrity

Section 1.

Treasury structure:

  • (a) A Federal Treasury shall be established for the finances of the federal government. Regions and Indigenous Nations may establish their own treasuries.
  • (b) Money raised or received by the Federal government shall be deposited in a general fund or special fund within the Federal Treasury. Money may not be withdrawn from the general fund or special fund except by law enacted through Federal Vote.
  • (c) Foreign financial assistance received by the Federal government shall be deposited in a Foreign Assistance Fund. Except where a particular distribution is required by the terms or special nature of the assistance, each Region and Indigenous Nation shall receive a share equal to the share of the Federal government and to the share of every other Region and Indigenous Nation.

Section 2.

Budget process:

  • (a) An annual Federal budget shall be proposed through the collaborative legislative platform and enacted by Federal Vote. The budget shall contain a complete plan of proposed expenditures, anticipated revenues, and other money available to the Federal government for the next fiscal year.
  • (b) Any citizen or rotating federal agency committee may propose budget items or amendments through the standard quarterly legislative cycle.
  • (c) Emergency appropriations may be enacted through the seven-day emergency voting process as prescribed in Article XI, Section 7.

Section 3.

Public Auditor:

  • (a) The Public Auditor shall be selected by Federal Vote from among candidates nominated through the collaborative legislative platform or by petition of citizens.
  • (b) The Public Auditor serves for a single term of 2 years. The Public Auditor may not serve consecutive terms, but may be elected to serve non-consecutive terms.
  • (c) The Public Auditor shall inspect and audit accounts in every branch, department, agency, or statutory authority of the Federal government and in other public legal entities or nonprofit organizations receiving public funds from the Federal government. Additional duties may be prescribed by law.
  • (d) The Public Auditor shall be independent of administrative control and shall report at least once every 12 months directly to all citizens through public reports made available on the federal governance platform. An increase of salary may not apply to the Public Auditor during the Public Auditor's term of office.
  • (e) The Public Auditor may be removed from office for cause by Federal Vote requiring a two-thirds majority. In that event, an acting Public Auditor shall be selected by lottery from among the other candidates who received the most votes in the most recent Public Auditor election, if such candidates remain available and eligible. If no such candidates are available or eligible, a special expedited Federal Vote shall be held to select an acting Public Auditor to serve until the next regular Public Auditor election.

Section 4.

Federal revenue structure:

  • (a) The Federal government shall derive revenue from the following sources:
    • (i) The Annual Citizenship Tax prescribed in Article VIII, Section 5, which serves both as a civic participation mechanism linking citizenship to shared responsibility and as a significant source of federal revenue;
    • (ii) Taxes on income as authorized by Article XI, Section 9(e);
    • (iii) Taxes, duties, and tariffs on imports as authorized by Article XI, Section 9(d);
    • (iv) The Federal share of net revenue from land leases, resource exploitation, and related activities as prescribed in Article XI, Section 11(c);
    • (v) The Federal share of revenue from navigation and shipping as prescribed in Article XI, Section 11(d);
    • (vi) Such other revenues as may be authorized by Federal Vote and consistent with this Constitution.
  • (b) The rate of the Annual Citizenship Tax shall remain as prescribed in Article VIII, Section 5 and may only be modified by constitutional amendment.
  • (c) Tax rates for income taxes, import duties, and other revenue mechanisms shall be established and adjusted by Federal Vote through the regular legislative process. Such rates shall be set with due regard for:
    • (i) Funding the services and obligations established by this Constitution, including education, health care, environmental stewardship, defense readiness, and digital and analog governance infrastructure;
    • (ii) The principle of progressivity, such that those with greater economic means contribute proportionally more;
    • (iii) The economic vitality of Regions and Indigenous Nations;
    • (iv) The revenue distribution requirements prescribed in Article XI, Section 11.
  • (d) No less than 10% of Federal revenue shall be allocated annually to the ecological stewardship obligations prescribed in Article VI, including environmental monitoring, climate adaptation, and support for Protected Natural Entity guardianship.
  • (e) The annual Federal budget prescribed in Section 2 of this Article shall include a comprehensive accounting of all revenue sources, projected receipts, and the allocation of funds across the obligations established by this Constitution.

ARTICLE XV - General Provisions

Section 1.

All parties to this constitution shall recognize the right of the people to life, liberty, happiness, education, health care, and legal services, and the right of Protected Natural Entities to ecological integrity, and shall take every step reasonable and necessary to provide these services and protections.

Section 2.

Radioactive, toxic chemical, or other harmful substances may not be tested, stored, used, or disposed of within the legal jurisdiction of this constitution without the express approval by Federal Vote and subject to the environmental protections prescribed in Article VI.

Section 3.

It is the solemn obligation of all parties to this constitution to uphold the provisions of this Constitution and to advance the principles of unity upon which this Constitution is founded.

Section 4.

A noncitizen, or a corporation not wholly owned by citizens, may not lease land or waters except as permitted by Regional or Indigenous Nation law and subject to the restrictions prescribed in this Constitution.

Section 5.

A lease agreement for the use of land for an indefinite term by a noncitizen, a corporation not wholly owned by citizens, or any foreign government is prohibited.

Section 6.

On assuming office, all persons serving in positions of public trust, including Public Auditors, members of Councils of Representatives, rotating committee members, and other appointed or elected positions, shall take an oath to uphold, promote, and support the laws and the Constitution as prescribed by law.

Section 7.

Enforcement of federal law:

  • (a) Regional police forces and judicial systems shall enforce federal law within their jurisdictions;
  • (b) Persistent refusal by a Region to enforce federal law constitutes a material breach of this Constitution;
  • (c) Upon determination by Federal Vote that a Region has persistently refused to enforce federal law, remedies may include:
    • Suspension of federal revenue sharing;
    • Temporary suspension of the Region's voting rights;
    • Authorization of enforcement by other Regions' personnel;
    • Suspension of the Region from the union;
  • (d) Such determinations require a two-thirds majority in Federal Vote and a simple majority of Indigenous Nations;
  • (e) A suspended Region may petition for readmission through the ratification process prescribed in Article XIX.

Section 8.

Economic framework:

  • (a) Private enterprise and market mechanisms shall operate within the framework of public land ownership established by this Constitution;
  • (b) Corporations and individuals may lease land and structures, own movable property and equipment, hold intellectual property rights, and engage in commerce subject to regulations prescribed by law;
  • (c) Financial capital, securities, intellectual property, and other non-real property assets may be privately owned and transferred;
  • (d) The Federal government shall regulate commerce to prevent monopolization, protect workers' rights, ensure environmental sustainability, and promote equitable distribution of economic benefits;
  • (e) International trade and investment shall be permitted subject to regulations that protect the principles established in this Constitution, including public land ownership and Indigenous Nations territorial rights.

Section 9.

Digital Infrastructure Resilience:

  • (a) The Federal government and all Regions shall maintain redundant internet infrastructure, including satellite-based backup systems, to minimize service disruptions to governance functions.
  • (b) Indigenous Nations shall have the right to manage and protect their digital resources and data sovereignty;
  • (c) Critical governance infrastructure shall be hardened against cyberattacks, electromagnetic pulses, natural disasters, and other potential threats as prescribed by law.
  • (d) Regular testing of analog backup systems shall be conducted at least twice annually at all levels of government, with results publicly reported.
  • (e) During any internet outage affecting more than 20% of citizens in a jurisdiction for more than 24 hours:
    • (i) Emergency protocols shall automatically activate;
    • (ii) The affected jurisdiction shall declare a state of infrastructure emergency;
    • (iii) All deadlines and voting periods shall be automatically extended as prescribed in Article XI, Section 13;
    • (iv) Public notification shall be made through all available non-digital means.
  • (f) Infrastructure emergency declarations shall be validated by Federal Vote once systems are restored, but actions taken in good faith during genuine emergencies shall remain valid.
  • (g) Investment in universal internet access shall be a continuous Federal priority, but the inability to achieve universal access shall not invalidate this Constitution or prevent governance from functioning through hybrid digital-analog systems.

ARTICLE XVI - Calendar

Section 1.

Fiscal year:

The fiscal year of the Federal government shall begin on January 1st and end on December 31st of each calendar year.

Section 2.

Federal holidays:

The following days are recognized as Federal holidays:

  • January 15: Civil Rights Day
  • February 15: African Heritage Day
  • March 15: European & West Asian Heritage Day
  • April 15: East Asian & Pacific Islander Heritage Day
  • May 15: Memorial Day
  • June 15: Independence Day
  • July 14: Decolonization Day
  • August 15: Diversity Day
  • September 15: Labor Day
  • October 15: Indigenous Heritage Day
  • November 25: Thanksgiving
  • December 25: Christmas

Section 3.

Quarterly legislative cycle:

  • (a) The quarterly legislative cycle prescribed in Article XI shall operate on the following schedule:
    • Quarter 1 Drafting Period: January 1 - March 31
    • Quarter 1 Voting Period: April 1 - June 30
    • Quarter 2 Drafting Period: July 1 - September 30
    • Quarter 2 Voting Period: October 1 - December 31
  • (b) Citizens must complete their minimum required voting by the last day of each voting period.

Section 4.

Law enactment process:

  • (a) Upon conclusion of a voting period, there shall be a two-week period during which any citizen may file appeals or claims regarding voting irregularities, election security, or validity concerns.
  • (b) If no appeals are filed, laws that received the required threshold of votes shall take effect at the conclusion of the two-week appeal period.
  • (c) Early enactment: If during a voting period a proposal receives sufficient affirmative votes such that the outcome is mathematically certain regardless of remaining votes, the two-week appeal period shall begin immediately. If no appeals are filed within those two weeks, the law shall take effect on the 15th day following the date the threshold was reached.
  • (d) If appeals are filed, an investigation must be conducted and concluded within three months. Laws under investigation shall not take effect until the investigation concludes and validates the vote, or a new vote may be called if irregularities are found.
  • (e) For Quarter 1 voting (ending June 30):
    • Appeal period: July 1 - July 14
    • If no appeals: Laws take effect July 15
    • If appeals: Investigation July 15 - October 14
  • (f) For Quarter 2 voting (ending December 31):
    • Appeal period: January 1 - January 14
    • If no appeals: Laws take effect January 15
    • If appeals: Investigation January 15 - April 14
  • (g) The body responsible for conducting investigations of voting irregularities shall be established by law.

Section 5.

Public Auditor election:

  • (a) The Public Auditor election shall be held during the Quarter 1 Voting Period (April 1 - June 30) of even-numbered years.
  • (b) Upon conclusion of the voting period, there shall be a two-week period during which any citizen may file appeals or claims regarding voting irregularities or election security.
  • (c) If appeals are filed, an investigation must be conducted and concluded by September 30. If irregularities are found that warrant a new election, such election shall be called immediately.
  • (d) Barring irregularities requiring a new election, the Public Auditor's term shall begin on October 1st following their election.

Section 6.

Annual Citizenship Tax:

The Annual Citizenship Tax prescribed in Article VIII is due on January 1st of each year, with the grace period extending to June 30th as prescribed in Article VIII, Section 5.

Section 7.

Regional, Municipal, and Local calendars:

Regions, Municipalities, Local governments, and Indigenous Nations may establish their own calendars, holidays, and legislative cycles, provided they do not conflict with Federal requirements under this Constitution.

ARTICLE XVII - Amendments

Section 1.

An amendment to this Constitution may be proposed by any citizen through the collaborative legislative platform in the manner provided by Article XI.

Section 2.

A proposed amendment shall become a part of the Constitution when approved by a simple majority vote in Federal Vote, subject to Indigenous Nations participation as prescribed in Article XI, Section 6.

Section 3.

Amendments to Article IV (Declaration of Rights) and Article VI (Ecological Stewardship) require a three-fourths majority vote in Federal Vote.

Section 4.

Emergency amendments may be proposed and voted upon using the seven-day emergency voting process prescribed in Article XI, Section 7, and require the same voting thresholds as specified in this Article.

Section 5.

Any amendment to this Constitution may itself be amended or repealed through the same process by which it was enacted.

Section 6.

At least every 10 years, the question "Shall there be a convention to revise or amend the Constitution?" shall be submitted to Federal Vote. If a majority of ballots cast upon the question is in the affirmative, delegates to the convention shall be chosen in the manner prescribed by Federal Vote.

ARTICLE XVIII - Transition

Section 1.

Treaty continuity: Treaties between existing States and Indigenous Nations or Tribes continue in effect except to the extent inconsistent with this Constitution, or as amended or repealed. Writs, actions, suits, proceedings, civil or criminal liability, prosecutions, judgments, sentences, orders, decrees, appeals, causes of action, defenses, contracts, claims, demands, titles, or rights continue unaffected except as modified in accordance with this Constitution.

Section 2.

Grassroots organization: The transition to governance under this Constitution shall proceed from the bottom up, through voluntary organization of neighborhoods, municipalities, and Regions.

  • (a) People residing in a geographic area may organize themselves into Neighborhoods meeting the requirements of Article IX, Section 2;
  • (b) Two or more Neighborhoods may agree to form a Municipality as prescribed in Article IX, Section 3;
  • (c) Two or more Municipalities may agree to form a Region as prescribed in Article IX, Section 4;
  • (d) Indigenous Nations may organize according to their own traditions and customs, consistent with the principles of this Constitution.

Section 3.

Joining the union: Once organized, a Region or Indigenous Nation may join this union through the ratification process prescribed in Article XIX.

Section 4.

Existing governmental obligations:

  • (a) When substantially all territory comprising an existing State has organized into one or more Regions under this Constitution, the rights, obligations, liabilities, and contracts of that State government shall be assumed by the Federal government or distributed among the successor Regions as determined by Federal Vote. Deliberate retention of nominal territory for the purpose of avoiding obligations under this section shall be void and without legal effect.
  • (b) Until a State's entire territory has organized under this Constitution, existing State and local governments may continue to operate under their current legal frameworks;
  • (c) People residing in areas not yet organized under this Constitution retain all rights under existing law.

Section 5.

Property transfer: When all territory of an existing State has organized under this Constitution, interests in property held by that State government may be transferred to the Federal government or distributed among successor Regions as determined by Federal Vote, for retention or use in accordance with this Constitution.

Section 6.

Local government transition: Local governments and their agencies may continue to exist even though their charters or powers are inconsistent with this Constitution, to promote an orderly transition. Mechanisms for resolving inconsistencies shall be determined by the relevant Regional government or by Federal Vote. This provision ceases to be effective 5 years after a Region's ratification of this Constitution.

Section 7.

Latter Nations recognition and organization:

  • (a) Cultural and political communities that identify as Latter Nations may organize and seek recognition as parties to this constitution through Federal Vote;
  • (b) A petition for recognition as a Latter Nation must demonstrate a defined cultural or historical identity as a distinct people, an organized governance structure capable of participating in Federal processes, and such other criteria as may be prescribed by law;
  • (c) The criteria and processes for Latter Nations recognition may be further refined by law enacted through Federal Vote;
  • (d) Once recognized, Latter Nations have the same voting rights and responsibilities as Indigenous Nations under this Constitution;
  • (e) Recognition as a Latter Nation does not automatically confer territorial rights. Latter Nations seeking territorial claims to specific lands must demonstrate documented connection through continuous presence, cultural practices, and stewardship, and obtain recognition from the Indigenous Nations with existing claims to those lands;
  • (f) Territorial claim petitions that are denied may not be resubmitted for the same territory for at least 2 years;
  • (g) Territorial claims may be recognized incrementally for different portions of claimed territory as agreements are reached with different Indigenous Nations.

Section 8.

Additional transition provisions: Additional transition provisions may be adopted by Federal Vote to address unforeseen circumstances and promote a smooth transition to governance under this Constitution.

ARTICLE XIX - Effective Date

Section 1.

This Constitution takes effect for any Region, Indigenous Nation, or Latter Nation upon ratification by a majority of voters in a referendum held among eligible voters within that jurisdiction.

Section 2.

The ratification referendum shall be conducted using secure voting methods, including offline facilities, and shall:

  • (a) remain open for voting for a period of not less than 30 days;
  • (b) require a minimum participation rate of 50% of eligible voters;
  • (c) be conducted with international observation to ensure fairness and transparency.

Section 3.

This Constitution does not fail if only a subset of potential jurisdictions ratify it. The union shall be formed by those jurisdictions that successfully ratify, with the expectation and invitation that additional jurisdictions may join over time.

Section 4.

Any Region, Indigenous Nation, or Latter Nation not part of the initial ratification may hold a ratification referendum at any time and shall join this union immediately upon successful ratification.

Section 5.

Upon ratification by at least one Region or Indigenous Nation, a transition period of one year shall commence, during which the structures and processes outlined in this Constitution shall be established for participating jurisdictions.

Section 6.

Initial election oversight:

  • (a) The first ratification referenda and Federal Votes conducted under this Constitution shall be observed and verified by international election monitoring organizations invited by the organizing communities;
  • (b) Until a permanent investigation body is established by law, allegations of voting irregularities in Federal Votes shall be investigated by temporary panels composed of:
    • Representatives from international election monitoring organizations;
    • Representatives from participating Regions and Indigenous Nations;
    • Independent technical experts in election security;
  • (c) Regions, Municipalities, and Neighborhoods shall establish their own processes for ensuring election integrity at their respective levels, subject to standards prescribed by Federal Vote;
  • (d) This temporary federal oversight structure shall remain in effect until replaced by permanent mechanisms established through Federal Vote, but no longer than two years from the first Federal Vote.

NOTES FOR FURTHER DEVELOPMENT

This Constitution addresses the core structural elements of governance but intentionally leaves certain implementation details to be determined through democratic processes. The following areas merit continued attention and development:

Implementation Challenges:

1. Voter Education at Scale:

  • How to ensure millions of citizens can make informed decisions on quarterly ballots with potentially hundreds of measures
  • Development of educational infrastructure, media literacy programs, and fact-checking mechanisms
  • Preventing misinformation and ensuring equitable access to information across diverse communities
  • Balancing comprehensive education with decision fatigue

2. Indigenous Nations Coordination:

  • Practical mechanisms for Indigenous Nations to coordinate voting on federal legislation
  • Timeline management when multiple Nations need to caucus on urgent matters
  • Dispute resolution when Indigenous Nations disagree on major cross-territorial decisions
  • Balancing participation demands with capacity constraints for smaller Nations
  • Implementation of territorial claims process for Latter Nations

3. Economic Transition:

  • Detailed mechanisms for converting existing property ownership to 99-year lease system
  • Managing the transition of corporations and financial markets to the new framework
  • International economic relationships and trade agreements under public land ownership
  • Ensuring economic stability during the transition period

4. Technology Infrastructure:

  • Ensuring universal, reliable internet access in rural and underserved areas
  • Maintaining and testing offline voting infrastructure alongside digital systems
  • Continuous security improvements as threats evolve
  • Accessibility accommodations for citizens with disabilities

5. Regional Compliance and Enforcement:

  • Edge cases where multiple Regions refuse to enforce the same federal law
  • Balancing federal supremacy with regional autonomy
  • Interstate cooperation mechanisms for complex enforcement scenarios
  • Role of Indigenous Nations in enforcement oversight

6. Ecological Implementation:

  • Establishing initial emissions reduction targets and review processes
  • Designating the first Protected Natural Entities and appointing guardians
  • Developing environmental impact assessment standards and independent review bodies
  • Integrating Indigenous ecological knowledge into formal environmental management systems
  • Defining thresholds for "irreversible ecological harm" across different ecosystem types

7. Constitutional Rights Guardian Panel:

  • Defining "demonstrated legal expertise" for Panel lottery eligibility
  • Establishing criteria distinguishing Rights-Protective from Standard certifications
  • Managing Panel workload if a high volume of judicial decisions involve constitutional rights
  • Ensuring the Panel does not become a bottleneck that delays justice
  • Balancing the Panel's independence with democratic accountability

Open Questions for Democratic Resolution:

These matters are intentionally left open for determination through Federal Vote:

  • Specific security protocols for the voting platform beyond baseline requirements in Article XI, Section 4
  • Detailed criteria for Latter Nations recognition beyond baseline requirements in Article XVIII, Section 7
  • Exact mechanisms for investigating voting irregularities after the initial transition period (Article XVI, Section 4(g))
  • Standards for local election integrity (Article XIX, Section 7(c))
  • Implementation details for voter education programs
  • Refinements to the economic framework as experience reveals challenges
  • Process details for Latter Nations territorial claims recognition
  • Specific emissions reduction targets and timelines under Article VI, Section 4
  • Criteria and procedures for designating Protected Natural Entities under Article VI, Section 2

This document is offered as an open-source starting point, inviting adaptation, critique, and improvement through collaborative deliberation.
